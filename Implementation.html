According to the World Health Organization, caries is the most common oral disease. It is estimated that 2.3 billion people, including 530 million children, suffer from this condition (Peres et al., 2019). Dental caries develops when bacteria, mainly Streptococcus mutans, accumulate on teeth, forming biofilm and metabolizing sugars. This results in production of different acids that can cause tooth demineralization (Rathee & Sapra, 2021). If untreated, this condition can lead to severe toothache, tooth abscess, decay, or even the loss of teeth (Cavities/Tooth Decay - Symptoms and Causes - Mayo Clinic, n.d.)

Sufficient oral hygiene and regular teeth brushing can significantly decrease the likelihood of dental caries. However, as around 1/3 of people suffer from caries worldwide, additional measures are necessary to improve oral hygiene. We conducted a public survey, which showed that a significant amount of people would be interested in an easier alternative tooth care, especially when having braces or while travelling.

Our answer to this problem is SALSASmile - a proteolytic enzyme solution that will detach the bacteria from the teeth by precisely cutting their anchoring points. The protease mixture can be in the form of a spray, liquid, or even chewing gum. From our survey we also found that people would prefer to have a mouthwash, paste or chewing gum to improve their oral hygiene. When stored in the right conditions, the protease can be kept in different forms

When looking for a suitable enzyme we conducted research on different candidates’ toxicity and medical applications. We gathered the results into a table that can be found on our Modeling page. One of the best candidates was the Atlantic cod trypsin that did not indicate toxicity, that has been previously used in biomedical studies (Gudmundsdóttir et al., 2013) and that showed a promising efficiency in kinetic modeling of SALSA degradation (please, refer to the Modeling page). The optimal working pH of trypsin (pH 7-9) (Lam et al., 2012) differs significantly from the acidic gastrointestinal pH which can reach 1.0-2.5 units (Evans et al., 1988). As trypsin is fully inactivated at pH 4 (Review of Proteins & Enzymes, n.d.), we can be sure that the protease will be inactive in the gastrointestinal tract. In the stability assay of trypsin in the intestinal juice with pH 6.1 it was found that less than 5% of the enzyme stays active after incubation in the intestinal juice for half an hour (Wohlman et al., 2016). However, there is a difference between acidic pH in the stomach and the neutral environment (pH 7) of the esophagus (Tutuian & Castell, 2006). As some amount of the enzyme could be absorbed in the esophagus and would need to be experimentally examined, an additional safety consideration would be the possible addition of non-toxic irreversible trypsin inhibitor α1-antitrypsin (NM & SG, 2007) that can fully inactivate the enzyme. This way, the enzyme activity is limited to a short period in the mouth, reducing the risk of cleaving additional proteins. As we suggest an innovative way for dental care, we need to consider other challenges, for example finding the most suitable form for our protease, and the efficiency and specificity of our final compound. Another challenge is the storage conditions for the enzymatic solution. Trypsin should be stored at the temperature around -20℃ to avoid autolysis (Trypsin from Porcine Pancreas Proteomics Grade, BioReagent, Dimethylated, n.d.). This raises a difficulty for correct product storage during transportation or at customer’s home. However, there are alternative approaches for storage of trypsin. It was found that upon lyophilization the trypsin can be stored at 2-8℃ for 2 years (Trypsin from Porcine Pancreas Proteomics Grade, BioReagent, Dimethylated, n.d.). Another possibility is keeping trypsin in an ammonium bicarbonate solution that can be stored at 2-8℃ for 2 weeks. Further, low pH supports trypsin storage, as this inactivates the autolysis activity of trypsin. Nevertheless, the surveys that we conducted as a part of our Integrated Human Practices both among the public and dentists showed a big interest and a need for a new approach for dental care.

We believe that our end users will be all people that need an efficient way to improve their oral hygiene. We envision that together with toothbrushing, our product will help keep teeth free from cariogenic bacteria, relieving people from the most common oral disease

Our project also develops a valuable research tool to engineer proteases with distinct specificity. We set up a method based on yeast two-hybrid where the proteolytic cleavage efficiency of a linker sequence between the DNA binding domain and the activation domain determines yeast growth rate. This method can be used for at least two purposes

First, it can be used to map the specificity determinants of distinct proteases. For this, a library of potential cleavage sequences can be introduced as the linker sequence between the DNA binding and activation domains, and when expressed together with the protease of interest, the cleavage efficiency is translated to yeast growth rate. The protease specificity determinants can be obtained by deep sequencing of the linker region. The sequences increasing in abundance will be the preferred substrates of the protease.

Secondly, as in our project, the method can be used to engineer proteases that cleave the desired sequence. In this case, either random mutagenesis or rational design is applied to the protease, followed by a competitive growth assay to select for the proteases that most efficiently cut the desired sequence. Alternatively, a library of various proteases can be used to screen for the best enzyme to cleave the desired sequence. The method can be widely used, as the ability to design proteases with distinct specificity would greatly benefit the field of synthetic biology.
