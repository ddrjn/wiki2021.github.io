For the experimental part of our project, we aimed to develop a high-throughput method in yeast to engineer proteases with distinct specificity and later to use the method to obtain a protease that would specifically cleave the SALSA protein.

Introduction

For this, we constructed a synthetic transcription factor where the DNA binding and activation domains are separated by an unstructured linker that contains a potential protease cleavage site. The synthetic transcription factor activates the expression of Sic1ΔN to inhibit yeast growth. However, when a protease cleaves the linker sequence, the two domains of the transcription factor are separated, the inhibitor is not expressed and the cells grow normally.

Overexpression of Sic1ΔN arrests yeast growth

We first set out to test the inhibition of yeast growth by inducing the expression of Sic1ΔN. Sic1 is an inhibitor of the master cell cycle regulator, Cdk1, that is targeted for degradation via phosphorylation of its N terminus, enabling cells to enter S phase (Kõivomägi et al., 2011). Previous studies have found that mutation of the phosphorylation sites in Sic1 leads to stabilization of Sic1 and a delay in cell cycle progression (Cross et al., 2007) (Figure 1).

We first tested whether Sic1, where the degradation motifs in the N terminus have been removed (Sic1ΔN), is capable of triggering efficient cell cycle arrest when overexpressed. For this, we cloned the coding sequence for Sic1 residues 90-284 to an expression vector with the LexA-dependent promoter. This expression cassette was transformed to a yeast strain expressing an estradiol-regulated synthetic transcription factor that regulates the LexA promoter (Ottoz et al., 2014).

To characterize the effect of Sic1ΔN expression on growth rate, we activated the LexA promoter at different levels using a range of estradiol concentrations. These experiments revealed a dose-dependent effect of Sic1ΔN on the growth rate. Therefore, using Sic1ΔN as a reporter gene allows to translate the activity of the transcription factor to growth rate. Importantly, this indicates that the setup can also be used to monitor the level of the transcription factor, enabling us to use it as a readout for the protease activity towards the transcription factor.

Figure 1. Dosing of transcription factor activity to growth rate via expression of Sic1ΔN. (A) The scheme of Sic1, showing the phosphorylation sites in the N terminus, necessary for Sic1 degradation, and the C-terminal part used in Sic1ΔN that inhibits Cdk1. (B) Induced overexpression of Sic1ΔN leads to dosage-dependent suppression of yeast growth.

The toxicity of proteases

Our protease engineering method potentially allows simultaneous screening of the proteases’ activity towards the target sequence in the LexA-linker-VP16 transcription factor and its activity towards essential cellular proteins. Overexpression of a protease with high activity and broad specificity is expected to have a negative effect on cell growth due to cleavage of cellular proteins. This would result in these cells gradually dropping out in the competitive growth assay, with the fastest dividing cells containing a protease that efficiently cleaves the LexA-linker-VP16 transcription factor to suppress Sic1ΔN expression, but does not cleave additional targets that would decrease the cell’s fitness.

To test our experimental setup, we first use TEV protease as the model enzyme, as it is highly studied and has a distinct cleavage site. To control the expression of the protease, we cloned TEV protease in an expression vector with GAL1 promoter that is inactive in glucose-containing medium, but highly expressed in galactose-containing medium.

The TEV expression plasmid was transformed to yeast and the effect of protease expression on cell viability was studied by comparing the yeast growth in the presence or absence of galactose. The assay showed that overexpression of TEV protease does not cause a growth defect. Due to the high specificity of TEV protease, this result could be expected, but it is a critical step in the high-throughput screening process involving proteases with very different substrate specificities.

The synthetic transcription factor containing protease site is functional

The final component of our protease activity screening assay is a synthetic transcription factor that contains the desired sequence for proteolytic targeting between the LexA DNA binding domain and the VP16 activation domain. We first needed to verify whether the transcription factor is able to efficiently activate Sic1ΔN expression from the LexA-dependent promoter. For this, the transcription factor was cloned to an expression cassette with GAL1 promoter and transformed to yeast that contains the Sic1ΔN reporter cassette.

For comparison, we used the estradiol-controlled transcription factor as above (Ottoz et al., 2014). Inducing the expression of the LexA-linker-VP16 transcription factor by addition of galactose results in a growth arrest of a similar efficiency as that triggered by the estradiol-regulated transcription factor. This experiment verifies that the created transcription factor is functional and indicates that the setup is flexible in terms of the sequence inserted between the transcription factor domains as the protease target sequence. Importantly, no growth defect was observed when the LexA-linker-VP16 expression was induced in cells that did not contain the Sic1ΔN reporter cassette, confirming the mechanism of the effect.

Protease-driven inactivation of transcription factor rescues yeast growth

As a proof-of-concept for this method, we first tested it with TEV protease, as it has a very specific cleavage sequence. For this, we used a LexA-linker-VP16 transcription factor, where the TEV cleavage site (ENLYFQS) was introduced to the linker. All three components of the assay (Figure 2) were transformed to yeast, followed by monitoring of yeast growth. Importantly, addition of the TEV protease rescued the growth arrest caused by LexA-linker-VP16-mediated expression of Sic1ΔN, presumably due to inactivation of the transcription factor by separating the DNA binding and activation domains. Next steps include validation of the cleavage of the transcription factor by TEV using Western Blot.

Figure 2. Transcriptional units in the yeast protease activity assay.

This provides a key proof-of-principle for the method. The method can next be used with a library of proteases or alternatively a library of linker sequences as a pooled input, allowing for selection of efficient cleavage pairs in a competitive growth assay in a high-throughput manner.

Next we aim to implement the method using trypsin and prolyl peptidase, as suggested by our modelling results. These proteases will be tested both as unmodified enzymes and also as fusions with the SRCR-binding peptide from S. mutans. The peptide is expected to dock the protease to the transcription factor, increasing the local concentration and driving the specific proteolytic cleavage. Further, we will create a library of protease mutants using error-prone PCR to find proteases with altered active site specificity to obtain an enzyme that is optimal for targeting the SALSA protein.
